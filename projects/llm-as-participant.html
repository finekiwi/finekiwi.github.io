LLM-as-Participant

Can synthetic personas replace real users in UX research? A systematic three-way comparison of LLM-generated feedback against developer and non-developer evaluations on a Korean welfare chatbot.

Problem & Motivation

User research is the backbone of human-centered design, but it remains one of the most resource-intensive stages. Recruiting participants, scheduling interviews, and analyzing transcripts can take weeks — creating a bottleneck that forces many teams to skip formative evaluation entirely or rely on developer intuition.

The emergence of LLM-based synthetic user research promises a faster alternative: generate synthetic personas, conduct simulated interviews, and extract usability insights in hours instead of weeks. Several recent papers have demonstrated promising results. But a critical question remains largely unexamined: how reliable are these synthetic insights, and under what conditions do they break down?

The research question: Rather than asking "Can LLMs replace real users?" — a binary that oversimplifies the problem — this study asks: "Where do LLM personas match real user feedback, where do they fail, and what systematic patterns characterize those failures?"

1

No Korean-language validation

Existing studies are overwhelmingly English-only. Korean has different politeness registers, indirection patterns, and welfare terminology that may affect persona fidelity.

2

Public welfare domain untested

Prior work focuses on consumer products and websites. Public welfare services involve complex eligibility rules, emotional stakes, and low digital literacy — a fundamentally different UX context.

3

No three-way comparison

Studies compare LLM vs. real users, but never distinguish developer users from non-developer users. Developer bias is a known confound in UX evaluation — ignoring it conflates technical literacy with real user behavior.

Study Design

The study compares UX feedback from three distinct groups on the same service using the same structured interview protocol. This three-way design isolates the effect of technical literacy (developer vs. non-developer) from the effect of synthetic generation (human vs. LLM).

Three-way comparison design. All groups evaluate the same welfare chatbot service using identical interview protocols, enabling direct comparison of feedback quality and coverage.

Hypotheses
H1

Surface-level match, depth mismatch

LLM personas will identify similar categories of pain points as real users, but underestimate severity for emotionally charged or context-dependent issues.

H2

Developer bias detection

Developers will over-report technical issues (latency, architecture) while under-reporting UX friction that non-developers experience (confusing language, trust concerns).

H3

Welfare-domain blind spots

LLMs will struggle with domain-specific patterns: welfare stigma, digital literacy barriers, and the emotional weight of eligibility rejection — experiences absent from training data.

H4

Systematic hallucination patterns

LLM-fabricated pain points will cluster around "generic UX best practices" rather than service-specific issues, revealing a tendency to default to conventional wisdom.

Methodology

The persona design follows the backstory-driven prompting approach established by Kapania et al. (CHI 2025), which demonstrated that rich narrative context produces more coherent and consistent LLM responses than attribute-list prompts. Each synthetic persona is defined across six demographic and behavioral variables, with two contrasting profiles designed to maximize coverage.

1

Backstory-driven system prompt

Each persona receives a structured system prompt containing a narrative backstory, specific life circumstances, and behavioral tendencies. This follows Kapania et al.'s finding that narrative framing produces more consistent persona behavior than attribute lists.

2

14-turn structured interview

The interview follows a fixed protocol: onboarding context (2 turns), task-based walkthrough of Welfare Compass v2 (6 turns), open-ended pain point elicitation (4 turns), and severity rating with rationale (2 turns). The same protocol is used for all three groups.

3

Data collection: pilot manual, production API

Pilot phase uses manual UI-based interviews to calibrate prompts and protocol. The main experiment will use API-based automated collection across 4 models for cross-validation — a decision driven by the need for systematic model comparison (see decision log).

4

Multi-model cross-validation

Each persona is interviewed across Claude 3.5 Sonnet, GPT-4o, and Gemini 1.5 Pro. Consensus across models strengthens confidence in a finding; divergence reveals model-specific artifacts. Pilot comparison determined the final model set.

Literature & Positioning

This study is positioned at the intersection of two active debates in the HCI community: the promise of LLM-augmented research methods and the growing critique of their uncritical adoption. Rather than advocating for or against synthetic personas, this work provides empirical evidence to ground the discussion.

2025

Simulacra of Stories

Kapania et al. — CHI 2025

Established backstory-driven prompting as the state-of-the-art approach for generating coherent LLM personas. Demonstrated that narrative context produces richer, more consistent responses than demographic attribute lists.

Method — adopted
2025

The Synthetic Persona Fallacy

ACM Interactions, 2025

Argues that LLM personas cannot represent real user diversity because they inherit training data biases and lack lived experience. Calls for empirical studies that measure, rather than assume, persona fidelity.

Critique — motivates this study
2024

Comparing Criteria Development with LLM Personas

ArXiv, 2024

Compared LLM-generated evaluation criteria against human-generated criteria for product assessment. Found overlap in high-level categories but divergence in specificity and contextual grounding.

Method — comparison framework reference
2025

The LLM-ification of CHI

CHI 2025

Meta-analysis of LLM usage across CHI submissions, documenting the rapid adoption of LLMs as research tools and participants. Raises concerns about methodological rigor and reproducibility standards.

Framework — contextualizes this work
2026

PersonaCite

AAMAS 2026

Proposes a citation-based framework for verifying LLM persona claims against source evidence. Introduces metrics for measuring persona grounding and attribution accuracy.

Framework — validation reference

This study's position: An empirical response to the theoretical critiques. Instead of debating whether synthetic personas should be used, we measure where they actually succeed and fail — providing actionable guidance for researchers deciding when LLM-based methods are appropriate.

Future Directions
  • Model tier comparison. Do frontier models (GPT-4, Claude Opus) produce meaningfully different persona behavior than mid-tier models? The multi-model design enables direct comparison of persona fidelity across model capabilities.
  • Incognito vs. informed personas. Testing whether personas that "know" they are being evaluated produce different feedback than those given no meta-awareness — probing the effect of evaluation context on synthetic behavior.
  • Cross-domain generalization. Extending the comparison framework to other public services (healthcare navigation, tax filing assistance) to test whether the patterns found in welfare generalize or are domain-specific.
